Thank you for going through this course. Putting in the time to learn something new — and actually finishing — takes discipline, and it genuinely means a lot that you chose to spend that time here. This course exists to help developers like you get real, practical Next.js skills without unnecessary fluff, and your feedback is what makes it better for every student who comes after you.

## Share Your Feedback

Honest feedback — positive or critical — is the most valuable thing you can give. Here's how to share it:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Leave a Comment on the YouTube Video">
    If you watched the video version of this course, scroll down to the comments and let the instructor know what you thought. Did a particular explanation click for you? Was there a section that felt confusing? Even a short comment helps shape future lessons and helps other learners decide whether the course is right for them.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Open an Issue or Discussion on GitHub">
    Found a typo, an outdated code sample, or a concept that could be explained better? Open an issue or start a discussion in the course GitHub repository. Pull requests with fixes are always welcome. The entire course is open source — your contribution could help thousands of future students.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Show Off What You Built">
    This one is the most fun. Share a link to your project — a GitHub repo, a live Vercel URL, or even a screenshot. The instructor genuinely loves seeing what students create. Your project is proof that the course works, and seeing real results from real people is the best motivation to keep improving the content.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Note>
  No feedback is too small. Even "this section was confusing" with no further detail is useful — it signals where to focus improvements.
</Note>

## Help Spread the Word

If this course helped you, consider sharing it with someone else who's trying to learn Next.js. Learning resources that are free, practical, and beginner-friendly are genuinely hard to find, and a personal recommendation from someone who's been through the material carries more weight than any algorithm.

Here are a few ways to spread the word:

- **Share the course link** on Twitter / X, LinkedIn, or in a developer Discord or Slack you're part of
- **Mention it in a blog post** — if you write about what you built or what you learned, link back to the course
- **Recommend it to a friend or colleague** who's expressed interest in React or full-stack development
- **Leave a star on the GitHub repo** — it helps the course show up in searches and signals to newcomers that it's worth their time

<Tip>
  The best time to share the course is right now, while it's fresh in your mind. You know exactly what a beginner needs to hear to take the leap — because you were that beginner not long ago.
</Tip>

Everything from here is yours to create.

Go build something worth sharing.
